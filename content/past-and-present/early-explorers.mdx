# Early Western Explorers

Long before sail and overland routes brought European-American
explorers to the Columbia and Snake rivers, the corridor was home
to many tribal nations whose presence on the water predates the
written record by thousands of years (see
[Tribal Communities](/past-and-present/tribal-communities/)).
The names below are a partial summary of the early non-Native
expeditions that documented the Northwest Discovery Water Trail
corridor between 1792 and 1811.

## Captain Robert Gray (1792)

The American merchant captain whose ship _Columbia Rediviva_
crossed the Columbia River bar on May 11, 1792, giving the river
its present English name.

- [Captain Robert Gray, HistoryLink.org](https://www.historylink.org/file/5051)

## Lewis & Clark Expedition (1803–1806)

The Corps of Discovery descended the Snake and Columbia in 1805
and returned upriver in 1806, charting the corridor and the
peoples already living along it.

- [Discovering Lewis & Clark](https://lewis-clark.org/)
- [Lewis & Clark Trail Heritage Foundation](https://lewisandclark.org/)

## David Thompson (1807–1811)

The British–Canadian fur trader and surveyor mapped the upper
Columbia drainage and reached the confluence of the Snake and
Columbia in July 1811.

- [David Thompson at the Snake/Columbia confluence, HistoryLink.org](https://www.historylink.org/file/5096)
