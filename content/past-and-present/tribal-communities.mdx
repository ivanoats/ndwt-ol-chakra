The Northwest Discovery Water Trail corridor is rich in
Indigenous heritage. The land along the Clearwater, Snake, and
Columbia rivers has been home to many tribal nations for
thousands of years; their traditional homelands extend far beyond
the borders of any present-day reservation. Each tribe has its
own origin, culture, history, and value system, with shared pride
in heritage and traditions and a deep relationship with the
land and water.

## Visiting tribal lands and events

Respect tribal property, privacy, customs, and ceremonies as you
would in anyone else's home. Many reservations host social and
cultural events such as tribal powwows and rodeos that are open
to the public. Some events and ceremonies require special
permission to attend, and others are restricted to tribal
members and practitioners. To find a calendar of public events
or to ask about attending, contact the relevant tribal office
directly. **Always ask permission before photographing
ceremonies or participating in tribal events.**

## Tribal sovereignty

Each tribe has an inherent right to govern actions within its
own jurisdiction. Sovereignty means tribes have the rights to
live freely and to develop socially, economically, culturally,
spiritually, and politically. Tribes have independent
governance, and states are precluded from interfering with a
tribe's self-government.

## Areas of special significance

Many places along the corridor are sacred ground. Be aware that
tribal people have occupied this region for thousands of years
and that landmarks you encounter may carry deep cultural and
spiritual significance.

- Do not disturb areas of sacred significance or places where
  there may be evidence of religious ceremony and practice.
- Do not disturb those engaged in spiritual ceremonies.
- Do not move or disturb offerings or other items left in
  specific places. These are spiritual offerings, not
  souvenirs.

## Tribal fisheries on the Columbia

Watch for tribal gillnets during open-fishery seasons between the
Umatilla River and Bonneville Dam. Nets are set from shore out
into the river — up to 300 feet — with a buoy marking the
downstream end. Some are anchored off old barges, bridges, or
sunken anchors, so stay alert during fishing season.

**In-lieu fishing sites** were built by the U.S. Army Corps of
Engineers to give tribal fishing families access close to
traditional fishing sites that were inundated when the dams were
built. You may also see traditional fishing platforms — wooden
scaffolds on stilts along the river's edge. These are
family-built and family-owned; do not walk onto them without
permission.

## Columbia River Inter-Tribal Fish Commission

The [Columbia River Inter-Tribal Fish Commission](https://critfc.org/)
(CRITFC) is a coordinating agency for the four Columbia River
treaty tribes — the **Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian
Reservation**, **Confederated Tribes of the Warm Springs
Reservation of Oregon**, **Confederated Tribes and Bands of the
Yakama Nation**, and the **Nez Perce Tribe** — and protects fish
of the Columbia River Basin and the treaty fishing rights of its
member tribes.

To learn more about the tribal nations along the Northwest
Discovery Water Trail corridor, contact each tribal office
directly or visit their websites.
