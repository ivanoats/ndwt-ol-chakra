# Trade & Industry

The corridor that today forms the Northwest Discovery Water
Trail has been a transportation backbone of the Pacific Northwest
for centuries. Indigenous trade networks moved goods up- and
down-river long before non-Native settlement; commercial steam,
rail, and barge traffic followed in the nineteenth century, and
the eight federal locks-and-dams between Lower Granite and
Bonneville have shaped both the economy and the river's flow
since the mid-twentieth century.

## Locks and dams

The eight Corps of Engineers navigation dams on the Columbia and
Snake provide hydroelectric power, deep-draft commercial
navigation as far inland as Lewiston, Idaho, and recreational
boating opportunities for the trail itself (see
[Lock & Dam Protocol](/river-navigation/lock-and-dam/) for
locking-through procedures).

- [Cascade Locks & Canal opens (1896), HistoryLink.org](https://www.historylink.org/file/7815)
- [Columbia River hydroelectric dams (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_River#Hydroelectric_dams)

## Celilo Falls

For more than 11,000 years, Celilo Falls was the largest
salmon-fishing site in North America and a major Indigenous
trading center. The falls were inundated when The Dalles Dam
went into operation in 1957.

## Steamboats and railroads

Sternwheeler traffic on the Columbia and Snake peaked in the
late nineteenth century before the railroads displaced it. A few
incidents from the era are documented in regional history
archives:

- [Steamboat explosion on the Snake River, HistoryLink.org](https://www.historylink.org/file/7722)

## Barge traffic today

Commercial tugs and tows still move grain, fertilizer, and other
cargo through the locks. Recreational boaters share the river
with these vessels — see
[Barge Traffic](/water-safety/barge-traffic/) for safety
guidance.
